Shadow City

A Cardgame About Light, Power, and the Night

This website presents an early-stage concept and reflects one phase of the design process. The final outcome is still in development and subject to change.

Research Questions

  1. Who controls the distribution of light in cities, and how does light become a tool of governance, surveillance, or exclusion?

    Urban lighting is rarely neutral. Decisions about where, when, and how intensely to illuminate a space often reflect broader systems of power, control, and governance—light can be used to shape human behavior, extend economic activity, and assert authority.

    • Light is used to shape behavior and perception:
      • Commercial districts stay brightly lit to extend consumption hours.
      • Street lighting design affects perceptions of safety and order.
      • Surveillance systems rely on constant illumination to enhance visibility.
    • Sometimes, the absence of light is also a political act — darkness is imposed to exclude, disempower, or neglect certain communities.
  2. Can a game-based framework critically reflect on the politics of light, visibility, and darkness?

    Games offer a structured space to simulate and reflect on real-world systems. Through mechanics, players can experience how illumination becomes a contested resource, and how visibility—or invisibility—shapes advantage, vulnerability, or resistance.

    • Actions such as "illuminate" or "dim" carry political consequences in the game world.
    • Darkness becomes not just a passive state, but an active zone of negotiation, resistance, or sanctuary.
  3. How might speculative gameplay help us rethink what a "right to darkness" might mean in the urban nightscape?

    In most urban contexts, darkness is framed as a deficiency—something to eliminate for safety, productivity, or visibility. But darkness can also be a shared right: a condition that supports rest, privacy, biodiversity, and even wonder. Stargazing, sleep quality, and nocturnal wildlife all depend on darkness, yet it is often compromised by overlighting and light pollution. A speculative game can open space to explore what it might mean to defend darkness as a public good, and how city policy might change if the “right to darkness” were treated as seriously as the right to light.

    • Darkness is not merely the absence of light—it can be a form of freedom, ecology, intimacy, and rest:
      • For astronomers, it's a condition for seeing the stars;
      • For wildlife, it's essential for survival;
      • For residents, it provides privacy and peace;
      • For cultural and spiritual life, it brings imagination, silence, and ritual.
    • Although this right doesn't currently exist in law, a speculative game allows players to experiment with its consequences and consider what policies might make it real.

Keywords

Light Pollution
Excessive or misdirected artificial light that disrupts ecosystems, human health, and the visibility of the night sky. Often a byproduct of over-illumination in urban environments.
Urban Governance
The policies, regulations, and power structures that shape how cities are planned, lit, monitored, and managed. Light becomes a tool through which governance is enacted.
Sensory Politics
The ways in which perception—especially sight, sound, and visibility—is controlled or shaped by social and technological systems. In this case, how light affects what can be seen or hidden in the city.
Critical Game Design
A design approach that uses gameplay to reflect on, critique, or challenge real-world systems. Here, game mechanics simulate conflicts over lighting, access, and urban space.
Nighttime Ecology
The study and protection of ecosystems that rely on natural darkness. Artificial light disrupts behaviors such as migration, reproduction, and feeding among nocturnal species.
Speculative Urbanism
The use of fiction, simulation, or design to imagine alternative futures for cities. This includes rethinking public rights, technologies, and spatial practices—such as the "right to darkness."

Situated Technology

  1. Power

    Light pollution is not merely a byproduct of infrastructure—it is an intentional distribution of visibility and obscurity that reflects uneven power structures.

  2. Perception

    Light shapes not just what is seen, but also how it is seen. The aesthetic of safety—bright, sanitized, uniform—has historically been linked to colonial, capitalist, and racialized regimes of visibility.

  3. Infrastructure

    Artificial lighting is an infrastructural technology that governs time, behavior, and territory. Yet, its governance is opaque.

Visual Representation

Main

Noir cinema and night photography
  • High-contrast black-and-white imagery, where deep shadows and isolated highlights intensify emotional atmosphere.
  • Ideal for evoking the sensory experience of the city at night, emphasizing psychological and spatial contrasts between light and darkness.
Cyberpunk/neon abstraction
  • Features saturated colors and strong contrasts, with neon lighting shaping a futuristic or hyper-urban aesthetic.
  • Useful for simulating the ambiance of artificial light spectra and sensory overload in late-capitalist nightscapes.

Secondary

Thermal/infrared visualizations
  • Uses false-color rendering to visualize heat or light radiation distribution.
  • Can be interpreted as a visual analogue for mapping light intensity or light pollution levels in urban environments.
Architectural lighting diagrams
  • Includes beam paths, illuminance distribution, and spatial penetration diagrams.
  • Serves as a visual backbone for interface prototypes or rule explanations in card-based or board games.

Proposal for drawing type

Layered Tactical Mapping
  • Overlays of light intensity, human density, ecological sensitivity, and surveillance zones across the city.
  • Inspired by Half-Earth Socialism (Including sketches), Civilization VI, etc.
  • Can include temporal dynamics (day–night transition, curfews, etc.).
Card-Mechanic Diagramming
  • Systems-based diagrams showing how card effects chain into urban transformations (e.g. illuminate → visibility → surveillance increase).
  • Cards as nodes, actions as edges—using tools like D3.js or Illustrator.
  • Useful to explain and critique systemic cause-and-effect in gameplay.
Speculative Urban Section Drawings
  • Cross-sections of urban areas rendered under different lighting policies.
  • Contrasts between old neighborhoods, commercial zones, ecological preserves, and "unlit zones."
  • Emphasizes social, emotional, and biological impacts of night lighting.
Sensorial Field Sketching
  • Loose, expressive sketches, collages, or visual diaries reflecting how different people experience visibility and darkness.
  • Ties into themes of affect, exclusion, and embodied perception.

A series of drawings illustrating key insights from the research
